Sri Lankan Leopard

Where to see them ,


    Yala National Park:Known for having the most densely populated leopard presence on earth
    Willpattu National Park:Once the most famous placefor seeing leopards in the country, sightings have been more common recently.
    Udawalawe National Park:A smaller but more tranquil option for those seeking to spot leopards without the crowds

    Sri Lankan leopard faces several threats:


    Poaching

    As human settlements expand, the natural habitats of leopards are increasingly fragmented
    reducing their living and hunting spaces.this also leads to Conflicts between humans and leopards, such as crop raiding and livestock predation, can lead to retaliatory killings .

    According to a report by **Global Voices**, poaching is a major threat to wildlife in Sri Lanka, with animals such as spotted deer
    and porcupines being among the most poached .
    The Sri Lankan leopard (Panthera pardus kotiya) is an endangered animal with an estimated population of 700-950 as of 2015 . Unfortunately, a number of leopards have also been killed using snares in the past few months .

    In Sri Lanka, animals are protected by the provisions of the Flora and Fauna Protection Ordinance. Poaching involves the illegal killing of animals,
    and it is a punishable offense . According to Jagath Gunawardena, an environmentalist, ecologist, and attorney-at-law, Sri Lankan legislature provides for the protection of animals, and although some animals can legally be killed, there are others that cannot .

    2.Habitat Loss and Fragmentation:

    As human settlements expand, the natural habitats of leopards are increasingly fragmented reducing their living and hunting spaces.this also leads to Conflicts
    between humans and leopards, such as crop raiding and livestock predation,
    can lead to retaliatory killings .

    Efforts to protect the Sri Lankan leopard include:


    1.Legal Protection

    The Fauna and Flora Protection Ordinance provides legal protection to the leopard,
    making it an offense to harm or kill a leopard



    2.Conservation Projects:

    Organizations like The Wilderness & Wildlife Conservation Trust (WWCT) are carrying out detailed studies on the leopard
    population and initiating several projects for their conservation.



    3.Community Engagement

    Conservation organizations collaborate with local communities,promoting coexistence
    and implementing measures to reduce conflict.
